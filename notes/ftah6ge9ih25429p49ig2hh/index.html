Country Space

Nomaler and Verspagen 2022

The authors propose the idea of a Country Space to complement The Product Space Conditions the Development of Nations.

Literature review

  • Product space introduced by The Product Space Conditions the Development of Nations
  • This paper extends product space in four ways:
    • The introduction of a "country space"
    • Testing the loss of comparative advantage
    • New measures of relatedness
    • Non-parametric test based on bootstrapping
  • Product space relevant because it links development and trade diversification, e.g. Coniglio et al. 2021, Hausmann and Rodrik 2003
  • However, diversification and specialization are different and even contradictory concepts: a country cannot have RCA in all products by definition
    • If new specializations are added, old ones will vanish
    • 59 countries have more losses than gains in RCA out of 155 from 2012 to 2018
    • China gained 367 but loss 224
  • Empirical evidence justifies product space
    • The Product Space Conditions the Development of Nations introduce the density measure and use a ttt-test to show that new specializations can be indicated by density; also conduct an OLS regress to justify the same point
    • This is followed by Boschma and Capone (2015, 2016); Bahar et al. (2017); Alsonso and Martin (2019) on Mexico; Guo and He (2015) on industrial employment; and Coniglio et al. 2021 using a non-parametric test showing that some countries fit the Product Space hypothesis while others less so

Methods

  • Product space measures:

    • RCA: for country iii and product jjj, RCA is defined
      xij=i’s share in exporting of jj’s share in world exportsx_{ij} = \frac{\text{$i$'s share in exporting of $j$}}{\text{$j$'s share in world exports}}xij​=j’s share in world exportsi’s share in exporting of j​

    • Product proximity:
      • Defined based on conditional probability: the proximity of two products iii and jjj is the probability that a country has RCA in iii given that it has RCA in jjj
      • This can be calculated in matrix form as follows:
        • kpqk_{pq}kpq​ is the number of countries with comparative advantage in both ppp and qqq
        • sps_psp​ is the number of countries with comparative advantage in ppp
        • Proximity ϕpq=kpqsp\phi_{pq} = \frac{k_{pq}}{s_{p}}ϕpq​=sp​kpq​​
        • Matrix of proximities Φ=S−1XXT\Phi = S^{-1}XX^TΦ=S−1XXT where SSS is the matrix with row sums of XXX on its diagonal and zeroes everywhere else
        • XXTXX^TXXT has elements equal to the dot product between the RCAs of ppp and the RCAs of qqq, i.e. the number of countries that share both RCAs
    • Density: the proximity of a country's current export basket to a product, calculated as
      dij=∑p ∈ exports of ixipϕpj∑p ∈ exports of iϕpjd_{ij} = \frac{\sum_{p\ \in \text{ exports of } i} x_{ip}\phi_{pj}}{\sum_{p\ \in \text{ exports of } i}\phi_{pj}}dij​=∑p ∈ exports of i​ϕpj​∑p ∈ exports of i​xip​ϕpj​​
      i.e. the average proximity to jjj of the products that iii has proximity in. This can be rewritten in matrix form as
      D=ΦXΦOD = \frac{\Phi X}{\Phi O}D=ΦOΦX​
      where OOO is a matrix of only ones.

  • New measures:

    • Mutually exclusive proximity
      • Measures which products a country is not specialized in
      • A country loses specializations at the same time it gains specializations
      • As such, some specializations are mutually exclusive: e.g. manufacturing and agriculture
      • If two products are mutually exclusive in specialization, then they are anti-competitive
      • Define the mutually exclusive proximity between products ppp and qqq as
        bpq=zpqupb_{pq} = \frac{z_{pq}}{u_p}bpq​=up​zpq​​
        where zpqz_{pq}zpq​ is the number of countries which export qqq but not ppp, and upu_pup​ is the number of countries which do not export ppp; this is equivalent to the matrix equation
        B=U−1ZXTB = U^{-1}ZX^TB=U−1ZXT
        i.e. ZXTZX^TZXT is the dot product between a row of ZZZ (which is O−XO - XO−X, whose elements are 111 if a country does not have RCA in a product and 000 otherwise), and a column of XTX^TXT, which is the RCA matrix, and U−1U^{-1}U−1 is as above.
    • Combining the mutually exclusive proximity and the regular proximity gives the new RCA indicator eije_{ij}eij​, between country iii and product jjj
      eij=1m(∑p ∈ exports of iϕjp+∑q ∈ non-exports of ibjq)e_{ij} = \frac{1}{m}(\sum_{p\ \in\ \text{exports of } i} \phi_{jp} + \sum_{q\ \in \text{ non-exports of }i} b_{jq})eij​=m1​(p ∈ exports of i∑​ϕjp​+q ∈ non-exports of i∑​bjq​)
      where mmm is the total number of products. This essentially says "average of product jjj's proximity with exports of iii, and product jjj's anti-proximity with non-exports of iii".
      • This can naturally be rewritten in matrix form as
        E=1m(ΦTX+BTZ)E = \frac{1}{m}(\Phi^T X + B^T Z)E=m1​(ΦTX+BTZ)
      • The columns of EEE sum to the original product ubiquity.
    • Define K=Φ−BK = \Phi - BK=Φ−B as the marginal proximities: the proximity between two products minus their antiproximities.
    • Thus we can also rewrite
      E=1m(BTO+KTX)E = \frac{1}{m}(B^T O + K^T X)E=m1​(BTO+KTX)
      i.e. the antiproximities of all products plus the marginal proximities to the exported products. The second term reflects the country's own specialization profile, while the first reflects an autonomous proximity true for all countries.

  • Country space

    • Products move through the "country space" simultaneously as countries move through the product space
    • Country space indicators can be obtained by transposing the ubiquity matrix SSS (ie. creating the diversity matrix)
    • A combined space can be produced and normalized by
      ETot=1r(m+n)(BTO+KTX+OB∗+XK∗)E^{Tot} =\frac{1}{r(m+n)}(B^T O + K^T X +OB^* + XK^*)ETot=r(m+n)1​(BTO+KTX+OB∗+XK∗)
      where B∗B^*B∗ and K∗K^*K∗ are the corresponding country space indicators for BBB and KKK, m+nm+nm+n is the number of products plus the number of countries, and rrr is the sum of the elements of XXX.

Non-parametric testing

  • Applied to data from 2012-2018, COMTRADE, 155 countries (> 1 million inhabitants)
  • 5197 product categories
  • For an indicator (proximity, antiproximity, EEE, country space, etc.) the bootstrap test is as follows:
    • Null hypothesis: the indicator being tested, on average, is the same for products that experienced a change in its RCA for countries from 2012 to 2018.
    1. Consider the matrix X−X0X - X_0X−X0​, where XXX is the RCA matrix in 2018 and X0X_0X0​ the matrix in 2012. An entry in this matrix is 1 if the corresponding product gained RCA bby a country.
    2. Call the set of all products in X0X_0X0​, the original RCA, the "potential gainers" - a total of NNN products. Divide into two parts: the products which actually gained RCA, and the products which didn't. Let the number of potential gainers which actually gained RCA be N1N_1N1​, and thus the non-gainers be N−N1N - N_1N−N1​.
    • Split the set of potential gainers into a set of size N1N_1N1​ and a set of size N−N1N - N_1N−N1​ 5000 times. Each time, find the average of the indicator for the set of size N1N_1N1​ and compare it to the average of the indicator of the actual gainers set.
    • Define
      p=no. of times actual average ≥ random average5000p = \frac{\text{no. of times actual average $\geq$ random average}}{\text{5000}}p=5000no. of times actual average ≥ random average​
      as the observed confidence value.
    • This can be run for both observed gains and losses in RCA, and for a specific product, a specific country, or all products and countries.
    • Big RCA changes are weighted the same as small RCA changes because small RCA changes are infrequent.

Results

  • Indicators have ppp-value <0.0001< 0.0001<0.0001 tested on all countries and products.
  • Indicators are far less effective on products (60% achieve p≤0.1p \leq 0.1p≤0.1)
  • Loss of RCA harder to predict than gain.
