Rich-Club Phenomenon

Bhattacharya et al.

Analysis of the weighted WTN reveals a log-normal distribution of scaled link weights; power-law growth of trade strenght with GDP holding true for all countries; "rich-club coefficient" analysis reveals that the size of the rich club (countries controlling top 50% of all trade) is shrinking. Simple dynamical model based on gravity model reproduces properties of WTN.

Methods and Results

  • Dataset is bilateral trade over 53 years
    • Number of links and nodes grew systematically
    • Link density roughly constant
  • Adjacency matrix used is eij+eji+iij+iji2\frac{e_{ij}+e_{ji}+i_{ij}+i_{ji}}{2}2eij​+eji​+iij​+iji​​ where eee is exports and iii is imports, to smooth ouut inconsistencies between ejie_{ji}eji​ and iiji_{ij}iij​; this graph is symmetrical
    • Distribution of weights broad; smallest less than 1M$1M\$1M$, largest more than 100000M100000M100000M$
    • Majority of links are small, with a few large links
    • Average weight per link grows from 15.54 million dollars to 308.8 million dollars in 2000
  • Probability density distribution of link weights
    • Log-log plots of link weight vs frequency have linear regions which are interpreted as a power law relationship for the PDF
    • However, more accurate fits are given by a log-normal distribution
      • Corroborated by Fagiolo et al. 2007 and Serrano 2007
  • Strength distribution of nodes
    • Define γ=∂log⁡s∂log⁡G\gamma = \frac{\partial \log s}{\partial \log G}γ=∂logG∂logs​ with GGG being GDP and sss being the strength of a country in the WTN. This results in the log-log relationship between sss and GGG, which is found to be positive and constant (i.e. a power law) for a single country over time
    • Distribution of γ\gammaγ for different countries:
      • 12 countries larger than 2 - these countries are countries born after the dissolution of the USSR (makes sense because new trade relationships, i.e. strength in the graph, was created faster than GDP was gained)

  • Rich club coefficient

Definition. Rich-club and rich-club coefficient.

  • A "rich club" is a subset of nkn_knk​ nodes whose degree values are at least kkk

  • The "rich-club coefficient" of these nodes is defined as

    ϕ(k)=2Eknk(nk−1)\phi(k) = \frac{2E_k}{n_k(n_k-1)}ϕ(k)=nk​(nk​−1)2Ek​​

    i.e. the density of the club.

  • Empirically, this definition has been shown to be insufficient: a randomly generated graph can occasionally display a rich-club effect

  • It is suggested that a maximally random network (MRN) with degrees equal to the WTN needs to be created, leading to a set of rich-club values ϕrandom(k)\phi_{random}(k)ϕrandom​(k)

    • Then, the ratio ρ(k)=ϕ(k)ϕrandom(k)\rho(k)=\frac{\phi(k)}{\phi_{random}(k)}ρ(k)=ϕrandom​(k)ϕ(k)​ is observed to check how significant the rich-club effect of the WTN is vs. a randomly generated control

  • The binary WTN does not display significant rich-club effects (ϕ≈1\phi \approx 1ϕ≈1, no difference from a random network)

  • The weighted WTN can have a similar rich-club coefficient defined as

    ϕ(s)=2∑i,jwijns(ns−1)\phi(s) = \frac{2\sum_{i,j}w_{ij}}{n_s(n_s-1)}ϕ(s)=ns​(ns​−1)2∑i,j​wij​​

    for the nsn_sns​ nodes above node strength sss.

Interpretation. The rich-club coefficient is a measure of the weighted density of the rich club.

  • Correspondingly, a MRN is generated with both kkk (degree) and sss (strength) preserved and with a symmetric weight matrix.
  • A self-iterative method is used:
  1. Arbitrary numbers to weights wijw_{ij}wij​ are assigned, maintaining that wji=wijw_{ji} = w_{ij}wji​=wij​.
  2. For every node iii, the difference δi=si−∑jwij\delta_{i} = s_i - \sum_{j} w_{ij}δi​=si​−∑j​wij​ is calculated (i.e. how far away the current node strength is from the desired node strength)
  3. Weights for iii are updated as wij→wij+δiwij∑jwijw_{ij} \to w_{ij} + \delta_{i}\frac{w_{ij}}{\sum_{j} w_{ij}}wij​→wij​+δi​∑j​wij​wij​​ such that iii has strength sis_isi​.
  4. Repeat the above until weights converge.

  • A MRN generated with this method again yields ρ(k)≈1\rho(k) \approx 1ρ(k)≈1, i.e. rich club coefficients of the WTN are insignificant; this does not mean that rich clubs do not exist, and can be explained by the fact that the MRN and the ITN share 85% of elements (nodes have same degree and strengths)

  • A rich club effect can be demonstrated by simply considering the proprotion (% of trade volume owned by rich club countries).

    • Top 19% of countries owned top 50% of trade in 1948; now 8% in 2000

  • Dynamical model based on gravity model.

    • Basic model is the gravity model of trade Fij=Gmimjlij2F_{ij} = \frac{G m_i m_j}{l^2_{ij}}Fij​=lij2​Gmi​mj​​; generalized to a parametric form

    3. Calculate magnitude of transaction by gravity model.
    4. Adjust GDPs based on transaction:

  • This dynamical model eventually converges to a stationary state with the average square GDP fluctuating around a mean.
